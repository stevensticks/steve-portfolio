Chapter 2 - 1964

Finding Joy

Earlier this week I watched the film Selma.(1) The Selma marches took place in Alabama only 50 years ago in 1965. 50 years ago, a very short amount of time, African-Americans were still fighting for fighting for their voice to be heard. In a film based on struggle and the murder and absolute shit that surrounds that struggle, there is one brief moment of sheer joy. It was when they found out a large group of musicians wanted to play a concert at the end of the march in Montgomery.The news was met with child like happiness.(2) During one of the most depressing times in human history music can uplift and inspire.

In 1964, 51 years ago the fight was for ending segregation, The Civil Rights Movement. Also in 1964, Otis Redding released his debut album, Pain in my Heart. A beautiful album featuring songs of love and love lost, one hell of a backing band and one of the best voices of all time. Redding born in Dawson, Georgia in 1941 spent the bulk of his teenage years touring the the southern states with Little Richard and Johnny Jenkins as a member of their backing bands, The Upsetters and The Pinetoppers, respectively. And while Pain in my Heart may not have dealt with the Civil Rights Movement directly, being from the south and touring the south at time when the colour of your skin meant you were only allowed to play certain clubs, I’m sure Redding knew exactly what he was doing. Making music to uplift people when they may be at their worst.

***

This in no way compares to my life, it would be foolish for me to think about trying to compare my little insignificant “White Canadian” problems to the struggles of the Civil Rights Movement. So I will just say this: For as long as I can remember I turned to 3 things during shitty times in my life. Family, Friends & Music. They are things that will always be there to stand you back up when you’re at your worst.
